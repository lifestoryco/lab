He came from Lepe, a small Andalusian fishing town on the Atlantic coast of Spain — the same coast Columbus had departed from a century before. The town had grown rich and salt-stained on tuna, sardine, and the trade winds. Boys born in Lepe in the late 1500s grew up watching ships return from a New World their grandparents had not lived to see.

Bernardo crossed in the great Habsburg outflow of the early seventeenth century, when Spain was still pouring soldiers, priests, and settlers into the silver provinces of New Spain. He earned his sergeant's stripes in the colonial army and put down roots at Saltillo — the high desert capital of Coahuila, the staging ground for every northern expedition to follow, and the town from which, ninety years later, his great-grandson Nicolás would ride into Texas.

Every Coy who would ever stand on Texas soil descends from this man.

We have no portrait of Bernardo. The records that survive are administrative — muster rolls, parish registers, a notary mention or two. But Saltillo's seventeenth-century archives place him there as sergeant, and his line is unbroken from that point. From Bernardo to me is sixteen generations. From Bernardo to my children's children, eighteen.
