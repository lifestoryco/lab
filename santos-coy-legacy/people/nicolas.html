Mission Founded

San Francisco de los Tejas (refounded), Concepción de los Hainai, and four others.

In April 1716, a column of seventy-five men, twelve priests, and several thousand head of livestock left Saltillo bound northeast — across the Rio Grande, across the Brazos, across the Trinity, into the pine country of the Tejas. The French, in Louisiana, had begun to push west. The Spanish Crown answered by founding a chain of six missions in East Texas. The expedition was commanded by Capt. Domingo Ramón, and on its muster roll appears the name Nicolás de los Santos Coy.

He was a soldado de cuera — a "leather-jacket soldier," named for the multi-layered cowhide coat that turned arrows. He carried a lance, an escopeta (short musket), a sword, and a small round shield. He rode a horse trained to stand under fire. The leather jackets were the standard cavalry of the Spanish frontier from Sonora to Florida, and Nicolás was one of them.

He is the first person in our line who walked on Texas dirt. Every Texan in the family — every Ranger, every gunfighter, every Bastrop schoolteacher and Houston engineer — descends from this man's willingness to ride into pine country with a lance.

The Ramón Expedition founded six missions and one presidio. They are gone now — the East Texas missions were abandoned in 1719 and refounded farther west — but the road they cut became the Old San Antonio Road, and that road defined Spanish Texas for the next century.
