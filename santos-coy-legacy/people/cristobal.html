Why this marriage matters

In 1731 the Spanish Crown shipped fifteen Canary Islander families to anchor a new villa at Béxar. They are San Antonio's founding civilian families. Curbelo was one of them. The marriage threaded the Coys permanently into San Antonio's First Families.

By the 1740s, Béxar had grown into a real frontier town — three groups of people pressed against each other on the same square mile of the San Antonio River: presidiales (the soldiers of the presidio), the Native and mestizo families of the surrounding missions, and the Canary Islander civilians who had arrived in 1731 to found the formal villa of San Fernando. There was a church, a plaza, an acequia system, and — by 1746 — a school.

The school was Cristóbal's. Family tradition, supported by parish records, holds that he taught the first classroom of children in what would become Texas — a one-room school of mud-brick walls and oiled paper windows, drilling letters and the Catholic catechism into the sons and daughters of soldiers, missionaries, and Isleños. He is, by this account, the first schoolteacher in the history of Texas.

Three years later he married into the families that had founded the town his school sat in.

On a date in 1749 recorded in the parish register of San Fernando, Cristóbal married María Josefa Curbelo. The Curbelos were one of the original fifteen Canary Islander families sent in 1731 to anchor the villa. Their descendants are still in San Antonio today, and from this point onward, every Coy in our line carries Isleño blood.

A separate notation places Francisco de los Santos Coy — likely Cristóbal's brother or cousin — on patrol against frontier raids on July 4, 1776. The coincidence has always struck me. On the day thirteen colonies declared themselves independent of one empire, our family was riding patrol for another.
