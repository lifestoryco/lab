By the time Pablo came of age in the 1780s, the Coys had been in Texas and Coahuila for four generations. He took up arms for the Spanish Crown at Presidio San Juan Bautista, the gateway fort on the Rio Grande and the staging ground for nearly every Spanish entry into Texas since the 1690s.

In 1810 the Mexican War of Independence erupted south of the Rio Grande. By 1812 it had reached Texas as the Gutiérrez–Magee Expedition — a republican filibuster that briefly seized San Antonio and declared the first Republic of Texas in April 1813. The Spanish Crown answered with Gen. Joaquín de Arredondo. Pablo, on garrison at San Juan Bautista, was almost certainly among the royalist forces that marched north to crush the rebellion.

On August 18, 1813, on a sandy oak motte beside the Medina River, four hours of fighting destroyed fourteen hundred republicans. Pablo's name does not appear on the survivor rolls.

The Battle of Medina remains the bloodiest day ever to occur on Texas soil — bloodier than the Alamo, bloodier than San Jacinto, bloodier than Goliad. The dead were left unburied where they fell. A young officer on Arredondo's staff named Antonio López de Santa Anna watched. Twenty-three years later, that officer would lead a Mexican army to a place called San Jacinto, where Pablo's nephew Antonio Clemente Coy would charge against him.

We have no body. We have no marker. We have only the absence of his name from any record after 1813, and the family memory passed down for more than two centuries that "the one who served the Crown" did not come home from Medina.
