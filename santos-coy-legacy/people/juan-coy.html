A note on the record

The "33 kills" figure is from Robert E. Lee's biography (the standard source) and is contested. Karnes County court records confirm at minimum the Daileyville fight and several lesser indictments. Whatever the exact number, by 1886 he was the most-feared man in the brush.

He is the part of the family no one wanted to talk about. We are talking about him now because if we don't, the record gets distorted on his behalf — and Juan Coy deserves to be remembered as he was, not as legend would prefer.

He was born in 1842, the son of Antonio Clemente Coy — Texas Ranger, San Jacinto veteran — and his Lipan Apache wife. Two warrior lineages: the frontier cavalry of New Spain and the Lipan, the southernmost Apache. He inherited both, and put both to terrifying use.

By the 1870s the brush country south of San Antonio — the chaparral of Karnes, Atascosa, and Wilson counties — had become one of the most violent corners of the post-Reconstruction American frontier. Cattle barons fenced public range. Tejano families who had grazed those grasslands since Spanish times were squeezed out. Sheriffs took sides. Assassinations were business.

By 1880 he was a hired gun. By 1886 he was a legend. By 1892 he was dead.

He worked for the cattleman W. G. Butler, one of the South Texas trail-driving magnates whose herds went up the Chisholm Trail. On August 30, 1886, in the open street of Daileyville in Karnes County, Coy and several Butler men met the posse of Sheriff Lafayette "Fate" Elder. The exchange of gunfire that followed has been called the deadliest small-town shootout of the post-Reconstruction era. Several men died on the spot. Coy walked away.

Six years later, in 1892, he was finally shot dead in San Antonio. The exact circumstances are disputed — some accounts say a saloon, some say an ambush. He was fifty.

His life is the subject of the published biography The Life and Death of Juan Coy: Outlaw and Gunfighter, 1842–1892 by Robert E. Lee — the standard published source on him, available through interlibrary loan. The truer story, as I understand it, is that he was the dark mirror of his father: same blood, same nerve, opposite side of the law. Antonio Clemente got a Ranger Cross. Juan Coy got a grave.
