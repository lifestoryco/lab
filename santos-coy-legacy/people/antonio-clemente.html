Five flags in one lifetime

Spain (1799–1821) · Mexico (1821–1836) · Republic of Texas (1836–1845) · United States (1845–1861, 1865–1891) · Confederate States (1861–1865). Antonio Clemente lived under all five.

He was born under the Spanish Crown — the same Crown his uncle Pablo had died for at Medina — and lived ninety-two years to see five flags fly over Texas. By the time he was thirty-seven, he was charging across a marshy field east of present-day Houston with eight hundred ragged Texan volunteers and an old Cherokee war cry on his lips.

The Battle of San Jacinto took eighteen minutes. When it was over, Texas was independent, Santa Anna was a prisoner, and the geography of the continent had been rewritten. Antonio Clemente was on the muster roll.

His brother Trinidad Coy had ridden out of the Alamo as a scout for Travis weeks before — sent for reinforcements that never arrived in time. Trinidad survived. The men he left behind did not. The two brothers' war stories, told together at family tables for the next half-century, framed the whole revolution from inside.

Five years after San Jacinto, he was riding patrol with Jack Hays.

In 1841, Antonio Clemente joined the company of Capt. John Coffee "Jack" Hays — the most celebrated Texas Ranger captain of the Republic era and the man who first armed his Rangers with the Colt Paterson revolver, transforming frontier warfare overnight. Antonio served as scout and confidant to Hays, navigating the brush country between Béxar and the Rio Grande on patrols against Comanche, Mexican, and outlaw incursions.

And here the family story turns again. At some point in this period, Antonio Clemente married a Lipan Apache woman. Her name has not yet been recovered. The Lipan were the southernmost Apache band, and they had ranged the Edwards Plateau and the Rio Grande country for generations before any Spaniard saw it. Her blood, her people's knowledge of this land, and her language flowed into the line. Every Coy descendant born after this marriage carries Lipan ancestry.

Antonio Clemente died in 1891 at the age of ninety-two, having outlived almost every man he served with. In 2018 — a hundred and twenty-seven years after his death — the State of Texas posthumously awarded him the Texas Ranger Cross for his service under Hays.
