Generation VIII

Victoriano Coy (~1869 – 1954)

Son of Juan Coy and Manuela Ríos, Victoriano was born into the worst of the brush-country years and lived to see the automobile, the airplane, and the Second World War. He married Juanita Jiménez. He carried the family out of the outlaw frontier and into the twentieth century, and the simple fact of his eighty-five quiet years is, in its way, the most radical thing any Coy ever did.

Generation IX

Antonio Coy & María Gutiérrez — Bastrop, Texas

Their son Antonio married María Gutiérrez in Bastrop, Texas — a Lost Pines town on the Colorado River, founded as Mina in 1832 and renamed for the German colonist Felipe Enrique Neri, Baron de Bastrop. The family settled there for a generation. Bastrop is where my mother is from.

Generation X

María "Mary" Coy m. Mariano Rosales Sr.

Their daughter María — Mary, to anyone who knew her — married Mariano Rosales Sr. With this marriage, the surname shifts. After ten generations the name Coy drops behind and Rosales becomes the line's surname — but the blood, the stories, and the Texas dirt run through unbroken.

Generation XI · Today

Mariano Rosales Jr. — Houston, Texas

I live in Houston — fifty miles from the field at San Jacinto where my fifth-great-grandfather Antonio Clemente charged in 1836. My children play on grass that my ancestors patrolled as soldados de cuera. I am the steward of this archive. If you are reading this and you recognize a name, please write.

Sixteen generations since Spain. Ten unbroken on Texas soil. The line continues.
