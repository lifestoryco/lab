A Note from the Descendant

I grew up knowing only fragments. This is the whole.

✦ ✦ ✦

My grandmother told me we were Spanish. My grandfather told me we were Texan. Somewhere on a summer afternoon in Bastrop, an aunt mentioned, almost as an aside, that one of our ancestors had ridden with Jack Hays — and another had been hanged a few counties over. I was a child. I filed it away.

Years later, sitting in the Bexar Archives at the University of Texas, I pulled a brittle muster roll dated 1716 and found one of our names on it. Nicolás de los Santos Coy. A soldier of the Domingo Ramón expedition. The first generation of our family to set foot in what would become Texas. The fragments began to lock together.

What you'll read on this site is the result of that work. Where I had family memory I have kept it — but I have refused to pass anything on without a citation. Every name links to a source. Every date is footnoted. Every claim — the heroic ones and the ones we don't talk about at Christmas — is backed by a record someone, somewhere, can audit.

We were soldiers and schoolteachers, royalists and revolutionaries, Rangers and outlaws. We were Andalusian and Canary Islander and Lipan Apache. And for ten generations now, we have been Texan.

— Mariano Rosales Jr., Houston, 2026

Three Bloodlines

Where the family's blood comes from

Andalusian

Lepe, Huelva, Spain

The original line. Sgt. Bernardo de los Santos Coy was born in this Atlantic fishing town on the Spanish coast around 1590 and crossed to New Spain in the early 1600s. His descendants would carry the name into Saltillo, then Béxar, then Texas.

Lepe on Wikipedia →

Canary Islander · Isleño

via María Josefa Curbelo (m. 1749)

In 1731, fifteen Canary Islander families were sent by the Spanish Crown to anchor the new villa of San Fernando de Béxar — they are the founding families of San Antonio. Cristóbal Coy's marriage to Curbelo wove our line into theirs.

Canary Islanders — Handbook of Texas →

Lipan Apache

via Antonio Clemente Coy's marriage

The Lipan were the southernmost Apache, ranging from the Edwards Plateau to the Rio Grande. Antonio Clemente married a Lipan woman in the 1830s, and her blood — and her people's history with this land — flows in every Coy descendant born after.

Lipan Apache — Handbook of Texas →

Primary & Secondary Sources

Where this chronicle comes from.

  • Archive
    Bexar Archives, Briscoe Center for American History — University of Texas at Austin

    The single largest collection of original colonial-period Texas records (1717–1836). Muster rolls, marriage registers, and royalist military records used throughout this site.

  • Reference
    The Handbook of Texas Online — Texas State Historical Association

    The authoritative encyclopedia of Texas history. Cross-referenced for every event, place, and figure named on this site.

  • Book
    Robert E. Lee, The Life and Death of Juan Coy: Outlaw and Gunfighter, 1842–1892

    The definitive published biography of Juan Coy. WorldCat record links to library holdings.

  • Museum
    Texas Ranger Hall of Fame and Museum — Waco, TX

    Holds the Texas Ranger Cross records, including Antonio Clemente Coy's posthumous 2018 award.

  • Museum
    San Jacinto Museum of History — La Porte, TX

    Veteran rolls of the April 21, 1836 battle.

  • Reference
    Battle of Medina (1813) — Handbook of Texas

    The bloodiest battle ever fought on Texas soil. The royalist casualty rolls survive only in fragments.

  • Reference
    Domingo Ramón Expedition (1716) — Handbook of Texas

    The expedition that founded the East Texas missions and brought the Coy name into Texas.

  • Reference
    Canary Islanders / Isleños of San Antonio — Handbook of Texas

    Background on María Josefa Curbelo's people, the founding families of San Antonio (1731).
